Why this matters

Your OS thinks every app, every file, and every notification weighs exactly the same.

It doesn’t. Decades of research show three structural failures, each measurable, each compounding. Focus modes and site blockers are patches. The structure itself is the problem.

What the OS does wrong

Distraction is one click. Focus is fifteen.

Entertainment sits in the same dock, with the same weight, as the tools that pay your rent. The OS treats them as equals — your nervous system pays the difference.

How it works

Restructure the OS around projects, not applications.

Step 01 / 05

Every project gets a drawer.

A drawer is its own menu bar identity, its own dock, its own space. Pull one open and the desktop you see is only the one you need.

Step 02 / 05

Artifacts, not apps.

Don't shortcut to Figma — shortcut to the one Figma file that matters. Not Slack, the #atlas-launch channel. Not Drive, the brief.

Step 03 / 05

Drag anything into a drawer.

A file, a person, a chat, an app, a link. It renders in native form — doc thumbnails, avatars, chat previews — not an abstract icon.

A note from the makers

We built Drawers because we were tired of ending days exhausted without remembering what we’d actually done.

The research is clear: the brain budgets energy by predicting what’s next. Every task switch forces a fresh prediction. A day of fragmented work drains that budget long before the actual work does.

Existing tools treat symptoms. Focus modes nag, site blockers fight, Pomodoros count. None of them fix the thing underneath — that your OS sees every file and every app and every distraction as equal weight, floating on a single undifferentiated plane.

We don’t want to make you more productive. We want to give you back the feeling of closing a drawer and knowing the rest of the world can wait.

— the Drawers team · a small studio in Toronto
Questions

A few things people ask.

01

Is this a launcher? A desktop manager? A new Mission Control?

None of those. Drawers is an interface layer on top of macOS — it uses Apple's existing APIs (Spaces, menubar, Dock extensions) and coordinates them around projects. Nothing is replaced; it's additive.

02

Does it work with [app]?

Yes. Drawers doesn't integrate with apps — it organizes access to them. If the app runs on macOS and has files, links, or channels, it can live in a drawer.

03

What does it cost?

Free during private beta. At public launch, a one-time purchase with optional sync. No subscriptions.

04

Why is switching slow on purpose?

Because cheap switching is the whole problem. A half-second of intentionality stops the reflexive jump that fragments your day.

05

Is my data safe?

Drawers is local-first. Everything lives in ~/Library on your Mac. If you turn on iCloud sync, it uses your private iCloud container — we never see it.

06

macOS only?

For now, yes. Linux is on the wish list. Windows would take longer because its window model is fundamentally different.
